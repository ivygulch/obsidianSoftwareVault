About the Growth Team
The Customer Acquisition, Growth, and Engagement team (CAGE) is focused on streamlining and innovating around the new and returning user experience for Thumbtack. By adding value for users and meeting their needs, we can activate them as first time customers and convince them to return to Thumbtack to help solve future problems by ensuring their experience was a good one.
We accomplish this by focusing both on getting the fundamentals right around core experiences like login and auth, as well as by building new features that allow us to better understand user needs early in their journey to get them to the right place.
The challenge
Many of the sorts of changes we’re making are unproven, and as a data driven company, we prefer to run experiments that vet our hypotheses. As an engineer, balancing places we can be scrappy to test ideas quickly vs places we need to take more care to exercise engineering rigor and build things the right way is important. Given we have cross functional teams, if you like looking at user problems from a product or design perspective, you’ll be a great fit for this.

## Cover letter

Dear Thumbtack Growth Engineering team,

The line in your posting about balancing "places we can be scrappy to test ideas quickly vs places we need to take more care" describes the two modes I've alternated between for the past decade — and earlier this year I ran both at once. From February to May I took an iOS incident-management app for volunteer fire departments from a non-technical client's requirements to a shipped v1.0, solo, spec-first, owning the SwiftUI client and the Supabase backend end-to-end. During the same period I was the Principal engineer who had owned Walmart's iOS checkout platform — the app's final conversion step — where the standard was ~85% automated test coverage, 99.9%+ crash-free sessions, and instrumentation across ~90% of core flows, because a misstep there costs real revenue at millions-of-users scale. Knowing which of those postures a given change deserves is, as far as I can tell, the actual job you're describing.

CAGE's specific ground — login, auth, onboarding, and understanding users early in their journey — is work I've shipped recently. On McGraw-Hill's Sharpen study app I delivered the onboarding, authentication, and settings features, worked on the RevenueCat-based subscription paywall and purchase/restore flows including free-trial eligibility handling, and integrated five analytics tools so the product team could see what users actually did. That's the same shape as your team's mandate: fundamentals done right, plus measurement to learn from.

Your experiment-driven culture is also how checkout operated: every new feature rolled out behind a feature flag, and more than half launched as A/B experiments measuring customer and business impact before full rollout. Feature leads owned the measurement loop end-to-end — building in analytics and telemetry, updating the team's dashboards, and monitoring behavior through rollout — so "data-driven" was the default posture, not an aspiration. Your cross-functional expectations match how I've worked at every scale. At Walmart I collaborated with backend teams on GraphQL schema design for mobile-optimized checkout APIs, led multi-team release readiness on a frequently-deployed inner-sourced codebase, mentored 12+ engineers, and communicated status and risk to stakeholders as a matter of course — checkout is where everyone's projects converge, so proactively raising blockers was survival, not process.

On "explore new frameworks and discuss when (or when not) to use them": I evaluated AI-assisted Xcode plugins at Walmart and recommended against them as deficient for iOS workflows, then helped evaluate and integrate Builder.io connecting our design library with Figma — which engineering management adopted as the standard for new iOS view work — and trained 80+ engineers on it. Today I use Claude Code and Codex daily and mentor a six-engineer iOS team at CLEAR on the same judgment: these tools accelerate engineering decisions, they don't replace them.

I'd welcome a conversation about where CAGE is heading and what the first two quarters of this role look like. Thank you for considering my application.

Douglas Sjoquist
