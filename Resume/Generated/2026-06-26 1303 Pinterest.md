## Cover letter

Dear Pinterest iOS Hiring Team,

Your posting is unusually direct about two things I care about: building Pinner-facing iOS at a scale of millions, and collaborating with AI in a way that keeps the engineer accountable for the result. Those are the same two threads my last several years have been about, so this is a targeted application, not a broad one.

On the iOS side: I spent 2021–2026 as Principal Software Engineer on Walmart's checkout platform — the highest-stakes flow in a flagship consumer app serving 5–6 million daily active users. I owned the architecture, drove it toward a unidirectional reactive design in Swift and Combine, held core flows at 99.9%+ crash-free sessions, and raised automated test coverage to ~85% with XCTest and XCUITest. Before that I carried consumer-scale iOS across Sam's Club (~2M DAU) and ASDA (500K+ DAU), where the work was squarely the "best practices in Objective-C and/or Swift" the posting names — migrating large Objective-C codebases to Swift without interrupting service, and converting app architecture to reactive patterns. The "build robust frameworks others rely on" line maps to real work: I defined and enforced iOS engineering standards — architecture, performance, security, testing — across a 120+ iOS engineer organization, and built the reusable frameworks (quiz/test-bank engines, a configurable conference-app framework reused across events) behind a string of independent client apps.

On AI collaboration: this is where I'd push back on the typical resume, because your bar is specifically about *critical evaluation, verification, and not over-relying on the tools* — and that's exactly how I work. My consistent framing is that agentic tools are accelerators for engineering judgment, not substitutes for it. At Walmart I helped evaluate and integrate Builder.io (connecting the design library and Figma to generate iOS views), trained 80+ engineers on AI-assisted workflows through live sessions, and sat on the working groups that decided which tools cleared the bar for org-wide adoption — which is fundamentally an exercise in verifying AI output before trusting it. Currently, at CLEAR, I use Claude Code and Codex to research a flagship iOS codebase and draft architectural proposals, then mentor a six-engineer team on bringing those tools in responsibly. And on FirefighterSpot, an incident-management app for volunteer fire departments, I built spec-first with GitHub Speckit and Claude Code while still personally owning the architecture, the Supabase backend, and the Swift Testing validation. In every case the deliverable is mine; the AI is the implementation partner I check.

A few of the other asks map cleanly: partnering end-to-end with design, product, and backend teams (I collaborated directly on GraphQL schema design and mobile-optimized APIs at Walmart checkout, and owned the backend outright on FirefighterSpot and TherexRx); rapid prototyping (FirefighterSpot went from non-technical customer requirements to a shipped v1.0 as a solo build); and communication (I've spoken on iOS testing and architecture at 360iDev and CocoaConf and led accessibility and testing training across Walmart's iOS org). One gap I'll name honestly: my scaling work has been architecture-and-reliability driven rather than formal A/B-test-driven — I've shipped to millions and instrumented heavily, but running A/B experiments as the primary iteration loop would be a new emphasis for me, not a established track record.

I'd welcome a conversation about the Product engineering teams and where my checkout-platform and consumer-scale background fits best. I can walk through any of the above in depth, including how I decide when to trust an AI-generated change and when to throw it out.

Best regards,
Douglas Sjoquist
