Dear Flighty Hiring Team,

I'm applying for the Sr. Full Stack Engineer role. Aviation and travel aren't a domain I would be ramping into — they are where I work right now. My current iOS engagement is with CLEAR, whose primary market is airline travelers: the flagship iOS app I'm enhancing syncs a user's calendar to detect upcoming flights and surfaces flight details from a backend FlightAware integration, and improving that traveler-facing experience is my main focus there. I've built aviation software before, too — between 2011 and 2013 I shipped and re-shipped the iOS apps for AOPA Aviation Summit, EAA AirVenture, SUN 'n FUN Fly-In, and Women in Aviation International. Flight tracking is its own problem, but the pull is the one your posting names: turning messy, inconsistent real-world aviation data into something a traveler can trust at a glance.

**On being the technical owner end-to-end.** My current solo engagement is the cleanest match to "there is no other team to throw it over the wall to." For FirefighterSpot — an iOS incident-management app for volunteer fire departments — I own the whole stack: I translated a non-technical client's requirements into formal specifications, implemented the SwiftUI client on Swift 6.2, built and operate the Supabase (Postgres) backend, and shipped a working prototype in roughly 30 days. Client, API, data, rollout, iteration — all mine. That is the shape of work your "What you'll do" list describes.

**On backend systems.** Before the last decade of iOS work I spent a decade as an independent Java/J2EE architect. The anchor was MetalsQMS, a six-year build of a quality-management system for Stillwater Mining (a platinum and palladium producer) — system architecture, Java implementation, an IBM DB2 production backend, and integration with their JD Edwards ERP for shipment intake, assay tracking, and settlement. Alongside it I delivered J2EE services across JBoss, WebSphere, and WebLogic, a configurable real-time engine-data display for a US Navy integrated-bridge system, and messaging-bus integrations on TIBCO Rendezvous. More recently: GraphQL schema design with backend teams for Walmart checkout and payments, and an AWS data lake plus push-notification infrastructure I built for McGraw-Hill's Sharpen app. One honest note — my JVM experience is Java, not Kotlin specifically. For a Swift engineer with a decade of Java architecture behind him, Kotlin is a short ramp rather than a new language family, but I'd rather you hear that from me up front.

**On SQL and datastores.** Strong SQL across IBM DB2, Oracle, Microsoft SQL Server, Apache Derby, and Postgres. On the NoSQL side, I architected the data-synchronization layer for the TherexRx physical-therapy app across an iOS client, a REST backend, and a Realm distributed datastore. I haven't run Cassandra or NATS in production; the relational and document-store fundamentals carry over, and a delay-prediction and notification pipeline is exactly the kind of problem domain I'd want to go deep on.

**On scale, performance, and reliability.** As Principal Software Engineer on Walmart's iOS checkout platform — the highest-stakes flow in a high-volume e-commerce app — I owned architecture and reliability across a 120+ iOS engineer organization: roughly 85% automated test coverage with a measurable drop in post-release defects, 99.9%+ crash-free sessions, and roughly 90% instrumentation on core checkout flows. Earlier I migrated the Sam's Club app from Objective-C to Swift with uninterrupted service for roughly 2M daily users. Latency, crash rate, and observability as standing commitments rather than cleanup projects — that's how I've worked.

**On product craft and design collaboration.** I've shipped 20-plus iOS apps across healthcare, retail, public safety, and aviation events, and at Walmart I led the 2025 evaluation and rollout of Builder.io — connecting the design library to Figma to generate iOS views — and trained 80+ engineers on it. I care how a feature feels, not only whether it is correct, and I would rather iterate directly with a designer than work from a finished spec.

**On the team and the working model.** A tiny, product-obsessed team with a high quality bar is the environment I have deliberately moved toward. My current engagements are solo (FirefighterSpot) and a six-engineer iOS team at CLEAR, where alongside the UX work I evaluate architecture and mentor the team on tooling. I'm based in the US Eastern time zone, so daily overlap with European and US Central colleagues is straightforward, and occasional travel for off-sites is no problem.

One more honest note: Flighty's stack uses the Composable Architecture. I have architected unidirectional, reactive iOS systems for years — the Sam's Club and Walmart checkout refactors were both unidirectional reactive designs — so TCA's model is familiar even though I have not used that specific library in production. I would expect to be productive in it quickly.

I'd welcome a conversation, and I'm glad to share the aviation conference apps and FirefighterSpot as portfolio context. Thank you for considering the application.
