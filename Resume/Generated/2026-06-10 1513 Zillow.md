About the role
This is not a standard Senior SDE role. We are looking for mobile product builders: engineers who can take a problem from an ambiguous idea to a shipped, instrumented, and continually improving mobile experience product. 
 
In this role, you will help define the problem, shape the solution with product and design partners, build across the iOS, Android and supporting services when needed, ship with strong operational discipline, measure what happened, and improve it over time. We’re looking for engineers who combine strong technical judgment with product sense, business context, and a modern AI-native way of working. AI tools are part of normal execution here—for exploration, coding, testing, debugging, and analysis—but ownership does not move to the tool. You remain accountable for quality, correctness, customer experience, and results. Success in this role looks like turning a fuzzy ask into a clear plan, making tradeoffs explicit, shipping with the right safeguards, and learning from what happens in production.

## Cover letter

Dear Zillow Touring Buyer Experience Team,

I'm applying for the Senior AI-Native Product Engineer, Mobile role. One line in your posting reads like something I've been saying for two years: AI tools are part of normal execution, "but ownership does not move to the tool." That's exactly how I work — I apply agentic tools across planning, specification, implementation, testing, and verification as accelerators for engineering judgment, not substitutes for it. Most postings treat AI tooling as a buzzword; yours describes a working style I already have, with the accountability clause I'd insist on anyway.

On AI-native as daily practice, not aspiration: I started down this road inside a large organization before it was fashionable. At Walmart, beginning in late 2024, I drove my own evaluation of AI development tooling for iOS, and through 2025 assisted the platform team in evaluating and integrating Builder.io — connecting Walmart's design library with Figma to generate iOS views. It was adopted as the standard for new iOS view work, and I trained 80+ engineers on AI-assisted workflows through live sessions plus recordings. Today the practice is deeper: on CLEAR's flagship iOS app I use Claude Code and Codex daily for codebase research, architectural proposals, code, and test development, and I mentor the six-engineer iOS team on folding those tools into their day-to-day work. And on a current personal project — a flight-history visualization app — I built and operate the full stack AI-natively: a Kotlin/Ktor + Postgres backend deployed to Fly.io and a SwiftUI client on the Composable Architecture, with the AI doing the typing and me owning every architectural decision, test, and production fix.

On ambiguous idea → shipped, instrumented, improving product: that's the through-line of my independent work — over 20 iOS apps from inception to the App Store across healthcare, retail, events, and real estate (including, fittingly, a sales tool for a high-end real-estate development back in 2014). The sharpest recent example is FirefighterSpot, an incident-management app for volunteer fire departments: I took a non-technical client's vision to a shipped version 1.0 — a local-first SwiftUI client on Swift 6.2 that synchronizes across devices, backed by a Supabase backend I built and operate — owning problem definition, tradeoffs, client, API, data, and rollout, then iterating with the client and fire-department stakeholders after launch.

On operational discipline at scale: for five years I was Principal Software Engineer on Walmart's iOS checkout platform — the highest-stakes flow in an app serving 5–6 million daily active users, inside a 120+ engineer iOS organization. I pushed our checkout team to reach and maintain roughly 85% automated test coverage with a measurable drop in post-release defects, and we held 99.9%+ crash-free sessions with around 90% instrumentation on core flows — so when something moved in production, we saw it and acted on it. I led multi-team delivery and release readiness on a large inner-sourced codebase with frequent production releases. Watching the signals and iterating wasn't a phase of the project; it was the job.

On partnering across product, design, and data: the Builder.io work was fundamentally a design-engineering partnership — design library, Figma, and code generation meeting in the middle. On McGraw-Hill's Sharpen study app I worked a product-team cadence owning onboarding, settings, and authentication, integrated five analytics tools, and built the data side too (an AWS data lake and push/email/SMS infrastructure). On CLEAR, enhancing the traveler-facing experience is my primary focus — feature work judged by how it feels to the customer, not just whether it's correct. And on strengthening teams: I helped define iOS engineering standards across architecture, performance, security, and testing for Walmart's whole iOS organization, mentored 12+ engineers, served as an iOS interview bar-raiser interviewing dozens of candidates a year, and taught accessibility reasoning and implementation techniques across the organization at the accessibility team's request.

One honest note on platform: my depth is iOS — fifteen-plus years of it, currently Swift 6.2, SwiftUI, and Swift Testing. I haven't shipped production Android. But I write Kotlin nearly every day right now (that personal project's backend is Kotlin/Ktor, built and deployed by me), so the language is already familiar, and a career of moving across platforms says the rest. "Strong depth in one platform and the ability or willingness to contribute to the other" describes me accurately, in that order.

I'm based in the U.S. Eastern time zone and have worked remote-first for years, so Cloud HQ fits how I already operate. I'd welcome a conversation. Thank you for considering the application.

Regards,
Doug Sjoquist
dwsjoquist@sunetos.com
