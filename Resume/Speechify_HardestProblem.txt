One of the hardest: helping take Walmart's iOS app from a proof of concept to production at Walmart's scale — where the core difficulty was speed. From the first lines of code in the spring of 2020 to live in production in the fall of 2021 in time for the holiday season, both the codebase and the team grew fast: from a single combined cart and checkout team of under ten engineers to a peak of six separate teams with more than thirty iOS engineers. A high-stakes flow, a fast-growing repo, and a fast-growing team coordinating with multiple backend teams growing at the same pace all at once is exactly the situation where architecture and quality usually rot.

I started on the small team doing the rewrite, and within a year I owned the architecture of the checkout platform — the highest-stakes flow in the app, serving five to six million daily users. It's deeply asynchronous and dependent on multiple services, so its worst failure modes — a declined payment, a network drop mid-transaction, a partial fulfillment — are the hardest to reproduce. The technical problem was keeping that flow correct, observable, and testable while all three things were moving: I anchored it on a unidirectional, reactive design (Swift and Combine) so state stayed deterministic, isolated dependencies so those failure paths could be tested without the live services, modularized with clear component boundaries so a growing team could work in parallel, and enforced standards and a test discipline that held coverage around 85% and crash-free sessions above 99.9%.

The speed is what made it hard. Any one of those is manageable alone; doing all of them while racing to a production deadline and onboarding engineers every few weeks is where most rewrites pile up the debt that sinks them. Reaching production in eighteen months without that — on an architecture the team kept building on for years — is the part I'm proudest of.
